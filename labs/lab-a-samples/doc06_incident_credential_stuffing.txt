Incident Report — Credential Stuffing Campaign Against CloudVault Storage

Between 2025-08-12 and 2025-08-19, CloudVault, a cloud storage provider serving approximately 4.2 million enterprise and SMB customers, experienced a sustained credential stuffing attack that resulted in unauthorized access to an estimated 18,400 customer accounts. The attack was detected by CloudVault's fraud engineering team on 2025-08-14 after automated systems flagged a sustained elevation in failed authentication attempts originating from a distributed set of residential proxy IP addresses.

Attackers used a credential corpus believed to originate from multiple prior data breaches, including the 2024 compromise of a business productivity platform. The stuffing infrastructure rotated across approximately 9,000 unique IP addresses during the campaign to evade rate-limiting controls. Authentication attempts peaked at 420,000 per hour on 2025-08-13. CloudVault's existing velocity-based throttling, calibrated to detect traditional brute-force patterns, did not trigger at the per-IP level due to the distributed nature of the attack.

Of the 18,400 accounts accessed, the attacker downloaded files from approximately 6,100 accounts and modified sharing permissions on 2,300 accounts to add attacker-controlled email addresses as collaborators, a persistence mechanism designed to maintain access even after password resets. CloudVault notified affected customers on 2025-08-15 and began forced password resets. MFA enrollment was made mandatory for all accounts on 2025-08-18.

Indicators associated with the campaign include the use of user-agent strings mimicking the CloudVault Android application version 3.1.2 (a version no longer in distribution), authentication requests exclusively from ASNs associated with residential proxy providers, and a consistent 2-to-4 second inter-request delay consistent with automated tooling with human-mimicry throttling. Defenders operating similar platforms should implement cross-ASN behavioral analytics in addition to per-IP rate limiting.
